Vegetation index Beirut map

I decided to study the vegetation in Beirut, since I wanted to look at a more urban area to see if the city has improved or reduced their greenery over the past few years as it is quite important to have greenery in heavily urban areas. I chose Beirut specifically because one of my friends is from these, so I thought it might be interesting. I also chose a quite large chunk of the map since I wanted to include the suburbs as well.

High quality imagery of the world from above is achieved through satellite remote sensing. Satellites take photos of parts of the map from above, which are then put together to make a whole image, which can be called an Orthomosaic. This map merges and seamlessly blends all of the images from the satellites. However, these images of the land can often be covered by clouds, which makes complicates the orthomosaic. So, to create a cloudless omposite from open data you need to filter the data by a time window, for which platforms like Google Earth evaluate images from all dates and determine the median value for each pixel in the orthomosaic, filtering out clouds in the process.

In this project, to study vegetation, we used multispectral satellite imagery, which captures a wide spectrum of light including both visible light (RGB) and invisible (Near-Infrared (NIR), Short-Wave Infrared). This wide variety of light, though not humanely visible, is incredibly useful in studying land cover, measuring water pollution, or mapping geological structures, since it captures what the human eye cannot. This can be directly linked to the UN SDGs since this type of imagery can aid in monitoring climate change effects, deforestation, or other valuable factors.

For this project we used imagery from Sentinel-2, which is a wide-swath, high-resolution, polar-orbiting Earth observation mission. Each satellite carried a single paload, which samples 13 spectral bands at spatial resolutions between 10-60 m. The mission is run by the European Space Agency as a part of Europe’s Copernicus Program. Data is continuously collected and made available to the public.

The aim of this project was to look at the vegetation difference over several years in our chosen location. A vegetation index is essentially multi-band spectral imagery transformed into a single layer, highlighting the abundance and health of vegetation in a specific area. Most commonly used is the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI). Plants absorb visible red light for photosynthesis, but the structure of healthy leaves also reflects NIR light so it does not overheat. NDVI makes this relationship into a scale from -1.0 to +1.0, where a positive 1 indicates dense, health vegetation, values near 0 indicate bare rock or soil or concrete, and anything close to a negative one represents water bodies.

Time of year is highly important for an orthomosaic since seasonality largely changes the NDVI of a place, since some seasons are much wetter or colder, while others are warmer and drier, affecting the vegetation growth and health. Thus I think for this task it is best to look at a mean from the whole year.

For my orthomosaic, I chose to look at the NDVI difference between 2017 and 2025, so I first took data from Google Earth from the whole year of 2017 and 2025 so I would get an average. Then in QGIS I used this data to create a true color composite for each of the year. Then I sidetracked a bit and experimented with false color composites, after which I created NDVI maps for each of the years. Lastly, I created the NDVI change layer by subtracting the two seperate NDVI layers.

In the NDVI change map we can see some small differences, most of the map shows no significant change. This was quite surprising to me since I expected more urban development, which would have caused a decrease in vegetation. There is some decrease across the map, shown in the dark reds, as it is all small scale and scattered, it does not quite catch attention.
